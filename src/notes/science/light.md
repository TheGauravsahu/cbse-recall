# Light — Reflection and Refraction Notes

This chapter explains how light behaves when it strikes mirrors and passes through transparent materials. It covers reflection, refraction, spherical mirrors, lenses, image formation, and numerical formulas.

---

# What is Light?

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects.

### Properties of Light
- Travels in a straight line.
- Travels with a speed of **$3 \times 10^8\ m/s$** in vacuum.
- Undergoes **Reflection** and **Refraction**.
- Does not require a material medium to travel.

---

# Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light into the same medium after striking a surface.

## Laws of Reflection

1. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.

$$
\angle i = \angle r
$$

2. The incident ray, reflected ray and normal lie in the same plane.

---

# Types of Reflection

## Regular Reflection

- Occurs on smooth surfaces.
- Forms a clear image.

Examples:
- Plane mirror
- Calm water

---

## Diffused Reflection

- Occurs on rough surfaces.
- No clear image is formed.

Examples:
- Wall
- Paper

---

# Spherical Mirrors

A spherical mirror is a part of a hollow sphere.

There are two types:

## Concave Mirror

- Reflecting surface is inside.
- Converging mirror.
- Can form real or virtual images.

### Uses

- Shaving mirror
- Dentist's mirror
- Headlights
- Solar furnace

---

## Convex Mirror

- Reflecting surface is outside.
- Diverging mirror.
- Always forms virtual and diminished images.

### Uses

- Rear-view mirrors
- Security mirrors

---

# Important Terms

## Pole (P)

Centre of the reflecting surface.

---

## Centre of Curvature (C)

Centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.

---

## Radius of Curvature (R)

Distance between P and C.

$$
R = 2f
$$

---

## Principal Axis

Straight line joining P and C.

---

## Principal Focus (F)

Point where rays parallel to principal axis meet (or appear to meet) after reflection.

---

## Focal Length

Distance between Pole and Focus.

$$
f = \frac{R}{2}
$$

Image is always

- Behind mirror
- Between P and F
- Virtual
- Erect
- Diminished

---

# Sign Convention (Cartesian)

Always remember:

- Light travels from Left → Right

### Object Distance

$$
u = \text{Negative}
$$

### Image Distance

- Positive for real image
- Negative for virtual image

### Focal Length

- Concave Mirror → Negative
- Convex Mirror → Positive

---

# Mirror Formula

$$
\frac{1}{f}=\frac{1}{v}+\frac{1}{u}
$$

Where

- $f$ = focal length
- $u$ = object distance
- $v$ = image distance

---

# Magnification (Mirror)

$$
m=\frac{h_i}{h_o}
$$

Also,

$$
m=-\frac{v}{u}
$$

Where

- $h_i$ = image height
- $h_o$ = object height

---

### Magnification Rules

Positive:
- Virtual
- Erect image

Negative:
- Real
- Inverted image

---

# Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another due to change in speed.

Examples

- Pencil appears bent in water.
- Pool appears shallower.
- Twinkling of stars.

---

# Cause of Refraction

Light changes speed while entering another medium.

- Air → Glass
  - Speed decreases
  - Ray bends towards normal

- Glass → Air
  - Speed increases
  - Ray bends away from normal

---

# Laws of Refraction

## First Law

Incident ray, refracted ray and normal lie in the same plane.

---

## Second Law (Snell's Law)

$$
\frac{\sin i}{\sin r}=n
$$

Where

- $i$ = angle of incidence
- $r$ = angle of refraction
- $n$ = refractive index

---

# Refractive Index

$$
n=\frac{\text{Speed of light in vacuum}}{\text{Speed of light in medium}}
$$

or

$$
n=\frac{c}{v}
$$

Higher refractive index means

- Light travels slower.
- Medium is optically denser.

---

# Optical Density

Optically denser medium slows down light more.

Example

Glass is optically denser than air.

---

# Refraction Through Glass Slab

- Ray bends towards normal while entering.
- Ray bends away while leaving.
- Emergent ray is parallel to incident ray.
- Lateral displacement occurs.

---

# Lenses

A lens is a transparent refracting medium bounded by two surfaces.

---

## Convex Lens

- Thicker at centre.
- Converging lens.

Uses

- Magnifying glass
- Camera
- Human eye
- Microscope

---

## Concave Lens

- Thinner at centre.
- Diverging lens.

Uses

- Spectacles for myopia
- Door viewers

---

# Principal Focus of Lens

Convex Lens

Parallel rays meet at focus after refraction.

---

Concave Lens

Parallel rays appear to diverge from focus.

---

# Optical Centre

The point through which light passes without deviation.

---

# Sign Convention for Lenses

Object distance

$$
u=\text{Negative}
$$

Focal Length

- Convex Lens → Positive
- Concave Lens → Negative

Image Distance

- Real → Positive
- Virtual → Negative

---

# Lens Formula

$$
\frac{1}{f}=\frac{1}{v}-\frac{1}{u}
$$

---

# Magnification of Lens

$$
m=\frac{h_i}{h_o}
$$

Also,

$$
m=\frac{v}{u}
$$

---

# Power of Lens

Power tells how strongly a lens converges or diverges light.

Formula

$$
P=\frac{1}{f}
$$

where focal length is in metres.

Unit

**Dioptre (D)**

---

### Positive Power

Convex Lens

---

### Negative Power

Concave Lens

---

# Combination of Lenses

Total Power

$$
P=P_1+P_2+P_3+\cdots
$$

# NCERT One-Line Revision

- Reflection is bouncing back of light.
- Refraction is bending of light.
- Concave mirror is converging.
- Convex mirror is diverging.
- Convex lens is converging.
- Concave lens is diverging.
- Rear-view mirrors use convex mirrors.
- Dentists use concave mirrors.
- Myopia is corrected using concave lens.
- Hypermetropia is corrected using convex lens.
- Real images can be obtained on a screen.
- Virtual images cannot be obtained on a screen.
- Object distance is always negative.
- Dioptre is the SI unit of power.

---

# Common Mistakes

> Forgetting the Cartesian Sign Convention.

> Using the mirror formula for lenses.

> Taking focal length positive for concave mirror.

> Forgetting to convert centimetres into metres while calculating power.

> Writing incorrect sign of magnification.
